## Random Variable

Given $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$, a $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}(\mathbb{Z}) \cup \{\pm \infty\}$ is called a **random variable** if $X$ is **measurable**.

$X$ is measurable is that $\forall a \in \mathbb{Z}, X^{-1}[a] \in \mathcal{F}$, in order to calculate the probability.

Stochastic process dives into sequences of random variables.

$\{X_i\}_{i \in \mathbb{N}}$ are mutually independent iff:

$$
\forall k \in \mathbb{N}, \forall S \in \binom{\mathbb{N}}{k}, \mathbb{P}\left[\bigwedge_{j = 1, i_j \in S}^k X_{i_j} =\text{(}\leq \text{)} a_j\right] = \prod_{j = 1}^k\mathbb{P[X_{i_j} =\text{(}\leq \text{)} a_j]}
$$

## $\sigma$-algebra and Conditional Expectation

The $\mathcal{F}$ is not always $2^\Omega$. $\mathcal{F}$ has such properties:  
- $\varnothing \in \mathcal{F}, \Omega \in \mathcal{F}$  
- $\forall A \in \mathcal{F} \Rightarrow A^c \in \mathcal{F}$  
- If $A_1, \ldots, A_k, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\displaystyle\bigcup_{i \geq 1}A_i \in \mathcal{F}$

Such $\mathcal{F}$ is called a **$\sigma$-algebra field**.

Here $\mathbb{P}: \mathcal{F} \to [0, 1]$, has such properties:  
- $\mathbb{P}[\varnothing] = 0, \mathbb{P}[\Omega] = 1$  
- $\forall A \in \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P}[A^c] + \mathbb{P}[A] = 1$  
- If $A_1, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$ and they are disjoint, then $\displaystyle \mathbb{P}\left[\bigcup_{i \geq 1}A_i\right] = \sum_{i \geq 1}\mathbb{P}[A_i]$
  
We denote $\sigma(X)$ be the minimum $\sigma$-algebra to make $X$ $\mathcal{F}$-measurable. $\sigma(X)$ is always exists, and for discrete $X$, $\sigma(X)$ can generate by divide $\Omega$ and generate by the rules before.

**$X$ is $\sigma(Y)$ measurable** ($\sigma(X) \subseteq \sigma(Y)$) iff **$X = f(Y)$ for some $f$** ($Y$ contains more information than $X$)

We define $\mathbb{E}[X | A] = \dfrac{\mathbb{E}[X\cdot \mathbf{1}_A]}{\mathbb{P}[A]}$

Also $\mathbb{E}[X | Y]$: Let $\mathbb{E}[X|Y = y_i] = f(y_i)$, then $\mathbb{E}[X | Y] = f(Y)$ is a random variable on $Y$.

On this definition, we notice that the value of $Y$ does not matter on $\mathbb{E}[X | Y]$. It only depends on the $\sigma$-algebra of $Y$

Then we let $\displaystyle\Omega = \bigsqcup_{n \geq 1}\Lambda_i$ where $\Lambda_i = Y^{-1}(y_n)$. So $\mathbb{E}[X | \Lambda_i] = \mathbb{E}[X | Y](y_n)$

So $\mathbb{E}[X | Y] = \mathbb{E}[X | \sigma(Y)]$

!!! normal-comment "Some properties"

    - $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X | \{\varnothing, \Omega\}]$  
    - Given ${G_1}, {G}_2, {G_1} \subseteq {G_2}$ are $\sigma$-algebras, then

    $$
    \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X | {G}_1]|G_2] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X|G_2]|G_1] = \mathbb{E}[X|G_1]
    $$

    specially, $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X | Y]] = \mathbb{E}[X]$

    - If $X$ is $G$ measurable, then $\mathbb{E}[X | G] = \mathbb{E}[X]$, i.e. if $X$ is $\sigma(Y)$ measurable, then $\mathbb{E}[X | Y] = X$ ($X = f(Y)$ so for some $Y = y_i$, $X$ is )

    If $X$ is $\sigma(Z)$-measurable, then $\mathbb{E}[X\cdot Y|Z] = \mathbb{E}[X]\cdot \mathbb{E}[Y|Z]$



## Convergence

Almost surely convergence vs. convergence in probability

**Almost surely convergence**

$X_n \overset{\text{a.s.}}{\longrightarrow}X$ iff.

$$
\mathbb{P}\left[\left\{\omega \ : \ \lim_{n \to \infty}X_n(\omega) = X(\omega)\right\}\right] = 1
$$

i.e. for **almost** all sample point, pointwise limit holds.

**Convergence in probability**

$X_n \overset{\text{P}}{\longrightarrow}X$ iff.

$$
\forall \varepsilon > 0, \mathbb{P}[|X_n - X| > \varepsilon] \to 0
$$

## Ball into Boxes Problem

### Coupon Collector

Suppose each box of a certain brand of cereal contains one of $n$ different types of coupons, chosen at random. How many boxes must one purchase in order to collect all $n$ types of coupons? -- In about $\Theta(n \log n)$

We model the problem by: how many balls should one throw into $n$ boxes to ensure each box contains at least one ball?

Let $Y_i:$ we have got $i - 1$ boxes full, how many times we throw balls to throw into a new box.

$$
\mathbb{E}Y = \mathbb{E}\sum_{i = 1}^n Y_i = \sum_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}Y_i
$$

And $Y_i \sim \text{Geom}\left(\dfrac{n - i + 1}{n}\right)$. So $\mathbb{E}Y = n\sum 1/k = nH_n = \Theta(n \log n)$.

## Concentration Inequalities

### Markov’s Inequality

$X \geq 0, a > 0$ 

$$
\mathbb{P}[X \geq a] \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}X}{a}
$$

(First Moment Method)

!!! remarks "Proof"

    $$
    X = X \cdot \mathbf{1}[X < a] + X \cdot \mathbf{1}[X \geq a] \geq 0 + X \cdot \mathbf{1}[X \geq a]
    $$

    so.

    $$
    \mathbb{E}X \geq a \mathbb{P}[X \geq a] + 0 \cdot \mathbb{P}[X < a]
    $$

!!! remarks "Indicator"

    $$
    \mathbf{1}[A] : \omega \in \Omega \to \begin{cases}
        1, \quad \omega \in A \\
        0, \quad \omega \notin A
    \end{cases}
    $$

    Then we can denote $X = X \cdot \mathbf{1}[X < a] + X \cdot \mathbf{1}[X \geq a]$

    Also, 

    $$
    \mathbb{E}\mathbf{1}[A] = \mathbb{P}[A]
    $$

    (Why?)

### Chebyshev’s Inequality

$a > 0$,

$$
\mathbb{P}[|X - \mathbb{E}X| \geq a] \leq \frac{\operatorname{Var} X}{a^2} \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}X^2}{a^2}
$$

(Second Moment Method)

!!! remarks "Proof"

    $$
    \text{LHS} = \mathbb{P}[(X - \mathbb{E}X)^2 \geq a^2] \geq \frac{\mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}X)^2]}{a^2} = \frac{\operatorname{Var}X}{a^2}
    $$

In coupons problem,

$$
\begin{aligned}
    \mathbb{P}[Y \geq nH_n + t] &\leq \mathbb{P}[|Y - \mathbb{E}Y| \geq t] \\
    & \leq \frac{\operatorname{Var} Y}{t^2} \geq \frac{n^2}{2t^2}
\end{aligned}
$$

And

$$
\operatorname{Var}Y = \sum_{i = 1}^n\operatorname{Var}Y_i = \ldots \leq \frac{1}{2}n^2
$$

However, in gaussian distribution, $\mathbb{P}[X \geq t] = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}t}\mathrm{e}^{-t^2/2} \ll \dfrac{\operatorname{Var}X}{t^2} = \dfrac{1}{t^2}$. So Chebyshev is not tight enough!

!!! remarks "Moment Generating function"

    $$
    M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{e}^{tX}] = \sum_{k = 0}^\infty c_k(t)\mathbb{E}X^k
    $$

    It can be understood as the **Laplace Transform** of $X$.

    $$
    M_X^{(n)}(0) = \mathbb{E}X^n
    $$

### Chernoff bound

$\{X_i\}_{i = 1}^n$ are independent, and $X_i \sim \operatorname{Ber}(p_i)$, let $\displaystyle X = \sum X_i$ and $\displaystyle\mu = \sum p_i$, Then

$$
\forall \varepsilon > 0,\quad \mathbb{P}[X \geq (1 + \varepsilon)\mu] \leq \left(\frac{\mathrm{e}^\varepsilon}{(1 + \varepsilon)^{1 + \varepsilon}}\right)^\mu \leq \mathrm{e}^{-\frac{\varepsilon^2}{3}\mu}
$$

$$
\forall \varepsilon \in (0, 1),\quad \mathbb{P}[X \leq (1 - \varepsilon)\mu] \leq \left(\frac{\mathrm{e}^{-\varepsilon}}{(1 - \varepsilon)^{1 - \varepsilon}}\right)^\mu \leq \mathrm{e}^{-\frac{\varepsilon^2}{2}\mu}
$$

This can be view as a quantitative version of the central limit theorem

!!! normal-comment "Proof"

    Using moment generating function:

    $$
    \mathbb{P}[X \geq (1 + \varepsilon)\mu] \overset{\forall \beta > 0}{=} \mathbb{P}[\mathrm{e}^{\beta X} \geq \mathrm{e}^{\beta (1 + \varepsilon)\mu}] \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{e}^{\beta X}]}{\mathrm{e}^{\beta (1 + \varepsilon)\mu}}
    $$

    And

    $$
    \begin{aligned}
        &\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{e}^{\beta X}] = \mathbb{E}\left[\prod_{i = 1}^n \mathrm{e}^{\beta X_i}\right] = \prod_{i = 1}^n \mathbb{E}\mathrm{e}^{\beta X_i} = \prod_{i = 1}^n (1 - p_i + p_i \mathrm{e}^\beta) \\
        \leq & \prod_{i = 1}^n \mathrm{e}^{p_i (\mathrm{e}^\beta - 1)} = \mathrm{e}^{\mu(\mathrm{e}^\beta - 1)}
    \end{aligned}
    $$

    So

    $$
    \mathbb{P}[X \geq (1 + \varepsilon)\mu] \leq \frac{\mathrm{e}^{\mu (\mathrm{e}^\beta - 1)}}{\mathrm{e}^{\beta (1 + \varepsilon)\mu}}
    $$

    Pick the optimal $\beta = \log (1 + \varepsilon)$ and we get the first inequality.

### Hoeffding’s inequality

$\{X_i\}_{i = 1}^n$ are independent, and $X_i \in [a_i, b_i]$, 

$$
\mathbb{P}[|X - \mathbb{E}X| \geq t] \leq 2 \exp \left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum_{i = 1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}\right)
$$

**Hoeffding Lemma**

$\mathbb{E}X = 0$ and $X \in [a, b]$, then

$$
\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{e}^{\alpha X}] \leq \exp \left(\frac{\alpha^2 (b - a)^2}{8}\right)
$$

## Examples

### Streaming Model

Given a infinite sequence and we can only see the former elements. In some time ($n$) there will come a request relevant to the former elements.

Now considering the plain task: answer how many numbers are there you have received.

To get the precise answer, the space complexity will be $O(\log n)$, which is increasing through time. What if we use a approximation algorithm?

A **random algorithm**: let $\sigma = <a_1, \ldots, a_m>$, which means that the answer should be $m$.  
Firstly init $X \gets 0$.  
Once $a_i$ arrives, let $X \gets X + 1$ in probability $2^{-X}$.  
Return $\hat{m} = 2^X - 1$

!!! normal-comment "Proof"

    $$
    \mathbb{E}[2^{X_k}|X_{k - 1}] = 2^{X_{k - 1}}\cdot (1 - 2^{-X_{k - 1}}) + 2^{X_{k - 1} + 1}\cdot 2^{-X_{k - 1}} = 2^{X_{k - 1}} + 1
    $$

    So

    $$
    \mathbb{E}[2^{X_k}] = \mathbb{E}[2^{X_{k - 1}}] + 1
    $$

    Also $\mathbb{E}[2^{X_0}] = 1$, so $\mathbb{E}\hat{m} = \mathbb{E}[2^{X_{m}}] - 1 = m$

However, the variance of $\hat{m}$ is not bounded!

So just run $t$ times of the algorithm and compute the average, we can reduce the variance to its $1 / n$. (**Average trick**)

Let $\displaystyle\hat{m}^* = \frac{1}{t}\sum_{k = 1}^t \hat{m}_i$, so

$$
\operatorname{Var}\hat{m}^* = \frac{\operatorname{Var}\hat{m}_1}{t} \leq \frac{m^2}{2t}
$$

So

$$
\mathbb{P}[|\hat{m}^* - m| \geq \varepsilon m] \leq \frac{\operatorname{Var}\hat{m}^*}{\varepsilon^2 m^2} \leq \frac{1}{2t\varepsilon^2} \leq \delta
$$

Better?

Let $\hat{m}^*_i$ be the average of $t$ $\hat{m}$. Let $\hat{m}^{**}$ be the medium of $\hat{m}^*_1, \ldots, \hat{m}^*_s$ (**Medium** exponentially increase the probability of landing near the expectation!)

Let $Y_i = \mathbf{1}[\hat{m}^*_i \text{ is good}]$, where $\hat{m}^*_i$ is good when $\hat{m}^*_i \in [(1 - \varepsilon)m, (1 + \varepsilon)m]$, $Y = \sum_{i = 1}^s Y_i$

WLOG let $\delta = 2/3$, then $\mathbb{P}[Y_i = 1] \geq \dfrac{2}{3} \Rightarrow \mathbb{E}Y \geq \dfrac{2}{3}s$. So

$$
\mathbb{P}[Y^* = 0] \leq \mathbb{P}\left[Y \leq \frac{1}{2}s\right] \leq \mathbb{P}\left[Y \leq \mathbb{E}Y - \frac{s}{6}\right] \overset{\text{Hoeffding}}{\leq} 2\exp\left(-\frac{s}{48}\right)
$$

### Multi-Armed Bandit

There are some bandits whose reward are of $\operatorname{Ber}(p_i) (p_1 > p_2 \geq \ldots \geq p_n)$. Let $X_t$ be the reward, and $a_t$ is the arm pulled at round $t$. Then we have $X_t \sim \operatorname{Ber}(p_{a_t})$. We want to maximize $\sum_{t = 1}^T \mathbb{E}X_t$

We introduce "**Regret**" value: 

$$
R(T) = T\cdot p_1 - \sum_{t = 1}^T \mathbb{E}X_t = \mathbb{E}\sum_{i = 1}^n \left(p_i - X_i\right) \geq 0
$$

and $\Delta_i = p_1 - p_i$

**Explore-then-Commit** (ETC) algorithm: explore some arms and get the **empirical mean**

1. Pull each arm $L$ times and get $\{Y_i^{(t)}\}_{t = 1}^{L} \to \hat{\mu}_i$ for all $i$ (Explore)  
2. Pull the best one in the remaining $T - nL$ rounds (Commit)

Let $Z_i^{(k)} = Y_i^{(k)} - Y_1^{(k)} \in [-1, 1]$, $Z_i = \sum_{k = 1}^L Z_{i}^{(k)}$. So $\mathbb{E}Z_i = -L\Delta_i$

Then 

$$
\begin{aligned}
    R(T) &= \underbrace{\sum_{i = 1}^n L \Delta_i}_{\text{First }nL\text{ pulls}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\sum_{i = 2}^n \mathbf{1}[\hat{\mu}_i \geq \hat{\mu}_1] \cdot (T - nL) \cdot \Delta_i}_{\text{Commits}} \\
    &= \sum_{i = 1}^n L \Delta_i + \sum_{i = 2}^n \mathbb{P}[\hat{\mu}_i \geq \hat{\mu}_1] \cdot (T - nL) \cdot \Delta_i \\
    &= \sum_{i = 1}^n L \Delta_i + (T - nL)\sum_{i = 2}^n \mathbb{P}\left[\sum_{k = 1}^L \left(Y_i^{(k)} - Y_1^{(k)}\right) \geq 0\right]  \cdot \Delta_i \\
    &= \sum_{i = 1}^n L \Delta_i + (T - nL)\sum_{i = 2}^n \mathbb{P}[Z_i \geq 0] \cdot \Delta_i \\
    &= \sum_{i = 1}^n L \Delta_i + (T - nL)\sum_{i = 2}^n \mathbb{P}[Z_i - \mathbb{E}Z_i \geq L\Delta_i] \cdot \Delta_i \\
    & \overset{\text{Hoeffding}}{\leq} \sum_{i = 1}^n L \Delta_i + (T - nL)\sum_{i = 2}^n \Delta_i \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta_i^2 L}{2}\right) \\
    & \leq \sum_{i = 1}^n L \Delta_i + T \sum_{i = 2}^n \Delta_i \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta_i^2 L}{2}\right) \\
    &\leq \sum_{i = 1}^n\left(L\Delta_i + T\Delta_i \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta_i^2 L}{2}\right) \right) \\
    &\leq \sum_{i = 1}^n\left(L + T \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta_i^2 L}{2}\right) \right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Let $g_i(\Delta_i, L) = L + T \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta_i^2 L}{2}\right)$

Our goal is $\displaystyle \min_L \max_{\Delta_i}g(\Delta_i, L)$ where $\min$ is the player's policy and $\max$ is the bandit owner's policy. (Choose $L$ before the owner choose $\Delta$)

By calculation we know that 

$$
g_i(\Delta_i, L) \leq g\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{L}}, L\right) = L + \frac{T\mathrm{e}^{-1 / 2}}{\sqrt{L}} \overset{L = \Theta(T^{2/3})}{\leq} \Theta(T^{2/3})
$$
